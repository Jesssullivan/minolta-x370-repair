# Focusing Screen Replacement

Procedure compiled from a Photrio mini-tutorial for the shared X-series mirror box (see `bibliography.md`). Minolta did not design this for routine end-user swapping — the screen is plastic and easily scratched or dropped into the mirror box if handled carelessly. Go slow.

## Tools

- Fine wooden toothpick, or a very fine jeweler's flat screwdriver
- Lens tissue
- Clean, well-lit, low-airflow work surface (a dropped screen is very easy to lose or scratch chasing it out of the mirror box)

## Procedure

1. **Remove the battery.** No power should be present while working near the mirror box.
2. **Lay a piece of lens tissue over the mirror** before doing anything else — this is your catch surface if the screen drops, and protects the mirror coating from tool contact.
3. **Orient the camera** so the mirror sits roughly horizontal and the lens mount opening is angled around 45°, so a dropped screen falls onto the tissue rather than off to the side.
4. Looking up into the mirror box from the lens mount, locate the **small retaining clip on the long side of the screen frame closest to the lens mount**, just inside the padding.
5. Gently pull/lift that clip with the toothpick or fine screwdriver — the screen frame releases and can be lifted out. Do not pry from the screen surface itself; work the frame/clip only.
6. Lift the old screen out by its frame edges only — never touch the ground/etched face.
7. Set the replacement screen in the same orientation (note any etched-side-up convention on your specific replacement listing — orientation matters for split-image alignment).
8. **Reinstallation:** gently press the new screen into the frame tab until the retaining nipple clicks into place. Don't force it — if it's not seating, recheck orientation before pressing harder.
9. Remove the lens tissue, reinstall the battery, and verify: mirror moves freely, viewfinder image is sharp edge-to-edge with no visible tilt or gap around the screen frame, and the split-image (if fitted) snaps cleanly with no double-image at focus.

## Cautions

- Do this in a single unhurried session — an interrupted mid-swap mirror box is not somewhere to leave the camera sitting.
- If the screen frame doesn't release easily, stop and re-examine rather than forcing — the clip is a small, easily-broken plastic tab on a 40+ year old camera.
- Aftermarket screens vary in brightness; if the finished image looks noticeably dimmer than before, that's a known complaint on generic aftermarket screens (see `parts-sourcing.md`), not necessarily a bad install.
